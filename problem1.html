Problem 1 - Double Quantum Dot & Aharonov-Bohm Effect

Interactive tight-binding model for a Double Quantum Dot (Landauer formalism)

Problem Assignment

The Double Quantum Dot (DQD) consists of two consecutive Double-Barrier Resonant Tunneling Diodes (DBRTD). The system is schematically represented by two localized orbitals \( A \) and \( B \) (for \( V_U = 0 \)).

  1. Derive analytical expressions for the Landauer conductance using the Fisher-Lee relation.
  2. Add a third orbital \( C \) \((V_U \neq 0)\) providing an additional tunneling channel. Introduce a magnetic field producing a flux represented by a phase \( 0 \leq \phi \leq 2\pi \) (singular gauge).
  3. Discuss interference effects (Aharonov-Bohm effect).
  4. Identify antiresonances and compare them with the antiresonance phenomenon in two coupled pendulums.
  5. Plot the transmission function for several parameter choices and discuss the physical behavior.

Reference: D'Amato, Pastawski, Weisz (PRB 1989)

Theoretical Derivation

1. From the Fisher-Lee relation to the Landauer conductance

We consider two single-level quantum dots:

  • Orbital \(A\)
  • Orbital \(B\)
  • Coupling: \( V_{AB}\)

The left (right) lead couples to \(A\,(B)\) via \( V_L\,(V_R) \).

The Hamiltonian is then:

\[ H = \left(\begin{matrix} \varepsilon_A & V_{AB} \\ V_{AB}^* & \varepsilon_B \end{matrix} \right) \]
Then, the Green's function is: \[ \begin{align} G &= \left[ \varepsilon I - H - \Sigma \right] ^{-1} \\ &= \left(\begin{matrix} \varepsilon - \varepsilon_A - \Sigma_L & -V_{AB} \\ -V_{AB}^* & \varepsilon - \varepsilon_B - \Sigma_R \end{matrix} \right)^{-1} \end{align} \] where \( \Sigma = \Sigma_L\ket{A}\bra{A} - \Sigma_R\ket{B}\bra{B} \) is the coupling to the leads at A (left lead) and B (right lead) respectively.

Taking \( a = \varepsilon - \varepsilon_A - \Sigma_L \) and \( b = \varepsilon - \varepsilon_B - \Sigma_R \) the Green's function takes the form:

\[ G = \left(\begin{matrix} a & -V_{AB} \\ -V_{AB}^* & b \end{matrix} \right)^{-1} \]

Which can trivially be inverted to get:

\[ G = \frac{1}{D} \left(\begin{matrix} b & -V_{AB} \\ -V_{AB}^* & a \end{matrix} \right) \]

where D is the determinant \( D = \text{Det}\{ \varepsilon I - H - \Sigma \} = ab - |V_{AB}|^2\)

Remembering the Fisher-Lee relation,

\[ T_{L,R}(E) = \text{Tr} \left\{ 2\Gamma_R G^R 2\Gamma_L G^A \right\} \]

and using the fact that the \( \Gamma \) matrices are diagonal in this case, we have:

\[ T_{L,R}(E) = 4 \Gamma_R \Gamma_L \left| G_{AB}^R \right|^2 \]
Substituting this result in the Landauer formula for the conductance \( G_{LR} \) (at \( T = 0K \)) we get: \[ \begin{align} G_{L,R} &= \frac{2e^2}{h} T_{L,R}(E_F) \\ &= \frac{2e^2}{h} \text{Tr} \left\{ 2\Gamma_R G_{AB}^R 2\Gamma_L G_{AB}^A \right\} \\ &= \frac{8e^2}{h} \Gamma_R \Gamma_L \left| G_{AB}^R \right|^2 \\ \end{align} \]

Using the explicit expression for the Green's function, we may identify the element \( G_{AB}^R = -\frac{V_{AB}}{D}. \) The resulting conductance, then, is:

\[ G_{L,R} = \frac{8e^2}{h} \Gamma_R \Gamma_L \left| \frac{V_{AB}}{D} \right|^2 \]

Finally, substituting \(D\), the full expresion is:

\[ G_{L,R} = \frac{8e^2}{h} \Gamma_R \Gamma_L \frac{|V_{AB}|^2}{\left| (\varepsilon - \varepsilon_A - \Sigma_L)(\varepsilon - \varepsilon_B - \Sigma_R) - |V_{AB}|^2 \right|^2} \]

2. Addition of a Third Orbital and Magnetic Flux

We now introduce a third localized orbital \( C \), providing an additional tunneling pathway between \( A \) and \( B \). The Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ H = \begin{pmatrix} \varepsilon_A & V_{AB} & V_{AC} \\ V_{AB}^* & \varepsilon_B & V_{CB} \\ V_{AC}^* & V_{CB}^* & \varepsilon_C \end{pmatrix} \]

A magnetic flux penetrating the loop formed by the three orbitals produces a phase factor in one of the hopping amplitudes (singular gauge choice). We choose the phase to reside in the \( C \to B \) link:

\[ V_{CB} \rightarrow V_{CB} e^{-i\alpha} \]

where

\[ \alpha = \frac{2\pi \Phi}{\Phi_0} \]

and \( \Phi_0 = h/e \) is the magnetic flux quantum.

3. Interference and the Aharonov–Bohm Effect

Transport between \( A \) and \( B \) now occurs through two coherent paths:

  • Direct path: \( A \rightarrow B \)
  • Indirect path: \( A \rightarrow C \rightarrow B \)

The total transmission amplitude may therefore be written schematically as:

\[ \mathcal{A}_{AB} = \mathcal{A}_{\text{direct}} + \mathcal{A}_{\text{indirect}} e^{-i\alpha} \]

Since the transmission depends on the modulus squared of the Green's function,

\[ T(E) \propto |\mathcal{A}_{AB}|^2 \]

we obtain an interference contribution:

\[ T(E) = |\mathcal{A}_{\text{direct}}|^2 + |\mathcal{A}_{\text{indirect}}|^2 + 2 \Re \left( \mathcal{A}_{\text{direct}}^* \mathcal{A}_{\text{indirect}} e^{-i\alpha} \right) \]

The last term produces oscillations of the transmission with period \( 2\pi \), which is the hallmark of the Aharonov–Bohm effect.

Constructive interference occurs when

\[ \alpha = 2\pi n \]

while destructive interference occurs when

\[ \alpha = (2n+1)\pi \]

4. Antiresonances

An antiresonance appears when the total transmission amplitude vanishes:

\[ \mathcal{A}_{\text{direct}} + \mathcal{A}_{\text{indirect}} e^{-i\alpha} = 0 \]

In this case, complete destructive interference suppresses transport, even though each individual path would allow transmission.

These antiresonances manifest as sharp transmission zeros in the numerical simulation.

They are analogous to the antiresonance phenomenon in two coupled pendulums: at certain driving frequencies one normal mode absorbs all energy while the other remains motionless.

5. Physical Interpretation

The magnetic flux does not modify the local energies of the orbitals. Instead, it changes the relative phase between quantum paths.

This demonstrates a purely quantum mechanical effect: the conductance depends on the enclosed magnetic flux even if the electrons never pass through a region of finite magnetic field. Only the total enclosed flux matters.

The oscillatory dependence of the conductance on \( \alpha \) confirms the topological nature of the Aharonov–Bohm effect.
